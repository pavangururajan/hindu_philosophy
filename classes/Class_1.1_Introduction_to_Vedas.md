## 1. The Architecture of Hindu Scriptures

Hindu philosophy is not based on a single book, but rather a vast library of texts that have evolved over millennia. To navigate this library, we must first understand its two primary categories: **Shruti** and **Smriti**.

### Shruti (श्रुति) - "That which is heard"
Shruti refers to the most authoritative, ancient, and foundational texts of Hinduism. These are considered *Apaurusheya* (अपौरुषेय), meaning "not of human origin." They were not authored by anyone, not even by God in the conventional sense, but are eternal truths "heard" or realized by ancient sages (Rishis) in deep states of meditation.

The core of Shruti is the **Vedas** (वेद), which means "Knowledge." There are four Vedas:
1. **Rigveda:** The oldest, consisting of hymns (mantras) praising various deities (Agni, Indra, Varuna).
2. **Yajurveda:** Contains prose mantras and instructions for rituals and sacrifices (Yajnas).
3. **Samaveda:** The Rigvedic hymns set to musical melodies; the foundation of Indian classical music.
4. **Atharvaveda:** Contains spells, charms, and practical knowledge for daily life, healing, and protection.

Each Veda is further divided into four sections, representing a progression from external ritual to internal philosophy:
* **Samhitas:** The core mantras and hymns.
* **Brahmanas:** Manuals explaining the rituals and their significance.
* **Aranyakas:** "Forest treatises" for hermits, transitioning from rituals to symbolic meditation.
* **Upanishads:** The philosophical culmination of the Vedas, dealing with the nature of reality, the Self (Atman), and the Supreme (Brahman). The Upanishads form the basis of Vedanta.

### Smriti (स्मृति) - "That which is remembered"
Smriti texts are of human origin. They are authored by sages to explain, elaborate, and apply the eternal truths of Shruti to specific times, places, and societies. While highly revered, if there is ever a contradiction between Shruti and Smriti, Shruti always prevails.

Key categories of Smriti include:
* **Itihasas (Epics):** The Ramayana and the Mahabharata (which contains the Bhagavad Gita).
* **Puranas:** Ancient lore, mythological stories, and genealogies of kings and deities (e.g., Bhagavata Purana, Vishnu Purana).
* **Dharma Shastras:** Law books and codes of conduct (e.g., Manusmriti, Yajnavalkya Smriti).
* **Agamas/Tantras:** Manuals for temple construction, idol worship, and specific sectarian practices.

## 2. The Prasthanatrayi (प्रस्थानत्रयी) - The Three Pillars of Vedanta

As Hindu thought evolved, the focus shifted from the ritualistic portions of the Vedas (Karma Kanda) to the philosophical portions (Jnana Kanda). The philosophical systems that emerged from the Upanishads are collectively known as **Vedanta** (Veda + Anta = "The end or culmination of the Vedas").

For any philosopher (Acharya) to establish a legitimate school of Vedanta (like Advaita, Vishishtadvaita, or Dvaita), they must write commentaries (Bhashyas) proving that their philosophy is supported by three foundational texts. These three texts are called the **Prasthanatrayi** (The Three Sources or Starting Points):

1. **Shruti Prasthana (The Starting Point of Revelation): The Upanishads**
   These are the direct, unmediated revelations of truth. They are the ultimate authority.

2. **Smriti Prasthana (The Starting Point of Tradition): The Bhagavad Gita**
   Part of the Mahabharata, the Gita is considered the essence of the Upanishads, made accessible to the common person. It synthesizes knowledge, action, and devotion.

3. **Nyaya Prasthana (The Starting Point of Logic): The Brahma Sutras**
   Authored by Sage Vyasa (Badarayana), these are concise, aphoristic statements (sutras) that logically organize and defend the teachings of the Upanishads, resolving apparent contradictions.

*Madhva Perspective Preview:* Later in Year 4, we will see how Sri Madhvacharya wrote brilliant commentaries on all three of these (his *Prasthanatrayi Bhashya*) to establish that the ultimate message of all scriptures is the absolute supremacy of Lord Narayana (Vishnu) and the eternal difference between the soul and God.

## 3. Key Sanskrit Verse

Let's look at a famous verse that beautifully summarizes the relationship between the Upanishads and the Bhagavad Gita. This is from the *Gita Dhyana Shlokas* (Meditation verses on the Gita).

**Devanagari:**
सर्वोपनिषदो गावो दोग्धा गोपालनन्दनः ।
पार्थो वत्सः सुधीर्भोक्ता दुग्धं गीतामृतं महत् ॥

**IAST Transliteration:**
*sarvopaniṣado gāvo dogdhā gopālanandanaḥ |*
*pārtho vatsaḥ sudhīrbhoktā dugdhaṃ gītāmṛtaṃ mahat ||*

**Word-by-Word Meaning:**
* *sarva* = all; *upaniṣadaḥ* = the Upanishads; *gāvaḥ* = the cows
* *dogdhā* = the milker; *gopāla-nandanaḥ* = the son of the cowherd (Krishna)
* *pārthaḥ* = Partha (Arjuna); *vatsaḥ* = the calf
* *su-dhīḥ* = the person of pure intellect; *bhoktā* = the enjoyer/drinker
* *dugdhaṃ* = the milk; *gītā-amṛtaṃ* = the nectar of the Gita; *mahat* = supreme/great

**Translation:**
"All the Upanishads are the cows, the milker is Krishna (the cowherd boy), Arjuna is the calf, men of purified intellect are the drinkers, and the supreme nectar of the Gita is the milk."

**Significance:** This verse perfectly illustrates that the Bhagavad Gita is not a new philosophy, but the distilled essence (the milk) of the vast and complex Upanishads (the cows), drawn out by Krishna for the benefit of Arjuna and all sincere seekers.

## 5. Vocabulary Additions (To be added to Glossary)

* **Shruti (श्रुति):** "That which is heard"; the revealed, unauthored scriptures (Vedas).
* **Smriti (स्मृति):** "That which is remembered"; authored texts based on tradition (Epics, Puranas).
* **Veda (वेद):** "Knowledge"; the four foundational texts of Hinduism.
* **Upanishad (उपनिषद्):** The philosophical portions of the Vedas; the foundation of Vedanta.
* **Prasthanatrayi (प्रस्थानत्रयी):** The three foundational texts of Vedanta (Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, Bhagavad Gita).
* **Apaurusheya (अपौरुषेय):** "Not of human origin"; a key characteristic of the Vedas.
